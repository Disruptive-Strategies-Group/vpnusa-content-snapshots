Our work

We work in the gap between awareness and law enforcement.

Awareness-raising is intended to reach as many people as possible to build resilience against extremist ideologies. Police step in once a threat is already clear. Where action is missing is when someone is turning toward violence and the people closest to them can feel it but don't know what to do.

That's where Violence Prevention Network steps in. We support three groups: the person at risk, the people around them, and the professionals who respond.

How our approach works

We start with the person.

People are drawn into extremism in an attempt to fulfill ordinary human needs: to belong, to matter, to make sense of anger or loss. Extremist groups exploit these needs and turn them toward hate and violence. We start by taking those needs seriously and building enough trust for change to become possible.

To understand a person in full, we use a structured framework our practitioners call Social Diagnostics, or GRIDD PRO. It looks past surface behavior at someone's life story, relationships, environment, unmet needs, and especially at their resources and strengths. From there we create a support plan together and track indicators of progress until violence becomes unlikely.

The method was built and refined over more than 25 years of practice, including work with over 5,000 people convicted of terrorism-related and violent extremist offenses.

Support and intervention

Most of our direct work is coaching and consultation.

We coach radicalized individuals, including those who aren't yet looking to leave. We also advise the people closest to them — a parent, teacher, counselor, or faith leader — who often notice a change first and want to help but aren't sure how.
